01♠ The Problem

Every card game online asks you to trust the dealer.

An invisible operator shuffles the deck, holds the money, and can see every card. In the UltimateBet & Absolute Poker scandal, insiders ran god-mode accounts that saw opponents' hole cards — robbing players for years.

The fix isn't "trust a better operator." It's a game where nobody can cheat — because nobody holds that power.

02♥ The Idea — Mental Poker, 1979

Can two strangers deal a fair hand with no dealer at all?

In 1979, Shamir, Rivest & Adleman — the R, S and A of RSA — asked whether two players who don't trust each other could play fair poker with no trusted dealer. Sealed Deck is the modern build of their answer.

"Can two potentially dishonest players play a fair game of poker without any mutually trusted intermediary?"

— Shamir, Rivest & Adleman, Mental Poker (1979)
03♣ The Padlock Dance

Two locks that open in any order.

Sealed Deck uses SRA commutative encryption. Each card is a number; each player's key is an exponent mod a large prime. Because exponents multiply, the locks commute — order doesn't matter.

Both players lock and shuffle the deck. To deal a card, the other player peels off only their lock — leaving one the owner alone can open.

04♦ Commit & Reveal

Lock the shuffle in before a card is seen.

Encryption keeps cards secret. Commit-reveal keeps the shuffle honest. Before any card is dealt, each player publishes keccak256(seed) — a fingerprint of their randomness — to the contract.

At showdown both reveal their seeds and the contract checks keccak256(seed) == commitment — the exact check anyone can rerun. The deal was fair, and you can prove it.

05♠ The Money — Trustless Custody

No house holds the pot. The contract does.

Both players stake into a PokerTable contract on Avalanche Fuji. The pot can move only four ways — and no single player can ever claim it alone.

Way Ⅰ

Cooperative settle

Both sign an EIP-712 Settlement. The contract verifies both signatures, then pays out.

Way Ⅱ

Fold

If a player folds, their opponent takes the pot immediately — fully on-chain.

Way Ⅲ

Timeout slash

Stall past N blocks and you forfeit your entire stake to the honest player. Can't freeze.

Way Ⅳ

Dispute void

No agreed winner? Either calls voidAndRefund — each gets their own chips back. Never steal.

An honest player always recovers their funds. The relay signs nothing and holds no key — it can never touch the money.

06♥ Buy-in — x402 on Avalanche

Pay the table in USDC, gaslessly.

The default table runs on native AVAX. Optionally, players buy in with Circle USDC over the x402 standard: an EIP-3009 authorization signed off-chain, settled straight into the contract.

Sign, don't spend

The buy-in is a signed X-PAYMENT header. No upfront gas, no approve transaction.

Straight to the table

The authorization's recipient is the table contract — the x402 primitive funds the pot directly.

USDC in, USDC out

Native Circle USDC on Fuji, the same token Avalanche x402 facilitators use. The pot pays in USDC.

10♦ Why It's Different

No house edge. No trusted shuffle. No peeking.

No house

No operator taking a rake or holding the pot. Two players, a contract, a powerless relay.

No one can peek

A private card carries two locks — neither player nor the server ever holds both.

Provably-fair deal

Commit-reveal binds the shuffle, then proves it — a check anyone can rerun.

The pot pays itself

Dual-signed, on-chain settlement; an honest player always recovers funds.

Can't be frozen

A timeout slash means a staller forfeits their stake — never a deadlock.

Verify everything

Every settlement is a Snowtrace transaction. Trust replaced by a link.
