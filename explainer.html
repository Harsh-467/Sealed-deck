The Problem

Every online card game asks you to trust the dealer.

When you play poker online, an invisible operator shuffles the deck, holds the money, and can see every card on the table. You are trusting that the shuffle was random, that nobody is peeking at your hand, and that the pot will actually be paid out. History says that trust gets abused.

In the UltimateBet & Absolute Poker "superuser" scandal, insiders used god-mode accounts that could see opponents' hole cards — stealing from some of the biggest names in the game, for years, before a leaked hand history exposed play that was simply too perfect to be human.

The fix isn't "trust a better operator." It's to build a game where nobody can cheat because nobody holds that power — not the other player, not the server, not the people who wrote it.

The Idea — Mental Poker, 1979

Can two players deal a fair hand with no trusted dealer at all?

The question is older than the internet. In 1979, Shamir, Rivest and Adleman — the R, S and A of RSA — asked whether two players who don't trust each other could play a fair game of poker "without using any cards and without any trusted intermediary." Their answer became the founding example of secure multi-party computation.

The trick is a special kind of lock. Sealed Deck builds the modern version of their protocol — two browsers shuffle and encrypt a deck between themselves, settle the money on a public blockchain, and a relay server in the middle never holds a key, never sees a card, and can never move a chip.

"Can two potentially dishonest players play a fair game of poker without any mutually trusted intermediary?" — Shamir, Rivest & Adleman, Mental Poker (1979)

How — The Padlock Dance

Two padlocks that open in any order.

Sealed Deck uses SRA commutative encryption. Each card is a number; each player's key is an exponent applied modulo a large prime. Because exponents multiply, the order you lock and unlock doesn't matter — A's lock and B's lock commute.

Picture a box with two padlocks. To shuffle, each player locks the whole deck with their own padlock and shuffles the order. Neither can read a card, because the other's lock is still on. To deal a card to one player, the other player simply removes their padlock — leaving exactly one lock that only the owner can open.

The Other Half — Commit / Reveal

Lock the shuffle in before any card is seen.

Encryption keeps cards secret. A second mechanism keeps the shuffle honest. Before a single card is dealt, each player publishes keccak256(seed) — a fingerprint of their shuffle randomness — directly to the smart contract.

Because the fingerprint is locked in first, nobody can change their shuffle after peeking at the opponent's. At showdown, both players reveal their seeds and the contract checks keccak256(seed) == commitment — the exact same check anyone can rerun. The deal was fair, and you can prove it.

The Money — Trustless Custody

No house holds the pot. The contract does.

Both players stake their buy-in into a PokerTable smart contract on the Avalanche Fuji C-Chain. From that moment the chips are governed by code, not by an operator. The pot can move only four ways — and no single player can ever claim it unilaterally.

🤝
WAY Ⅰ

Cooperative settle

Both players sign an EIP-712 Settlement agreeing on the winner and pot. The contract verifies both signatures match the two seats, then pays out. Neither can settle alone.

🃏
WAY Ⅱ

Fold

If a player folds, their opponent takes the pot immediately. The simplest, most common path — fully on-chain.

⏱️
WAY Ⅲ

Timeout slash

If a player stalls past N blocks, the other calls claimTimeout. The delinquent forfeits their entire stake to the honest player. The table can never freeze.

↩️
WAY Ⅳ

Dispute void

If both reveal but never agree on a signed winner, either may voidAndRefund after the deadline — each gets back exactly their own contribution. A griefer can force a void but can never steal.

The guarantee that matters: an honest player always recovers their funds — worst case, they win by waiting out a timeout, or get fully refunded by a void. The relay server signs nothing and holds no key; it can never touch the money.

Buy-in — x402 on Avalanche

Pay the table in USDC, gaslessly.

The default table runs on native AVAX. An optional path lets players buy in with Avalanche-native Circle USDC over the x402 HTTP-402 payment standard. The buy-in is an EIP-3009 transferWithAuthorization the player signs off-chain — no gas, no pre-approval — and the contract's joinWithAuthorization settles it straight into the pot.

✍️

Sign, don't spend

The player signs a payment authorization as an X-PAYMENT header. No upfront gas, no approve transaction.

🏦

Straight to the table

The authorization's recipient is the table contract — the x402 settlement primitive funds the pot directly.

💵

Real USDC, USDC payout

Native Circle USDC on Fuji, the same EIP-3009 token used by Avalanche x402 facilitators. The whole pot pays out in USDC.

Why It's Different

No house edge. No trusted shuffle. No peeking.

♠

No house

There is no operator taking a rake or holding the pot. Two players, a contract, and a powerless relay.

🔒

No one can peek

SRA commutative encryption means a private card carries two locks — neither player nor the server ever holds both.

🎲

Provably-fair deal

Commit-reveal binds the shuffle before any card moves, then proves it after — the same check anyone can rerun.

⛓️

The pot pays itself

Settlement is dual-signed and on-chain; an honest player always recovers their funds, worst case by timeout.

🛡️

Can't be frozen

A timeout slash guarantees a staller forfeits their stake — the game can never deadlock with your money inside.

🔍

Verify everything

Every settlement is a transaction you can open on Snowtrace. Trust is replaced by a link.
